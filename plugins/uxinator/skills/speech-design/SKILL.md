# Speech Design

Designs keynotes and conference talks built for provocation, not education. Produces a talk structure with one core argument, one anchor story, one emotional peak, and one memorable close. Tim brings the insight and stories — this skill provides the architecture that keeps him from drifting into training mode.

## How It Works

```
BRIEF (gather context)
    ↓
CORE ARGUMENT (one sentence)
    ↓
STORY SELECTION (anchor + supporting)
    ↓
EMOTIONAL ARC (open → build → peak → resolve → close)
    ↓
TALK STRUCTURE (timed, with delivery notes)
    ↓
THE ONE LINE (what they'll repeat tomorrow)
```

## Step 2: Define the Core Argument

The core argument is the ONE reframe the audience hasn't heard. Not a topic. Not a category. A specific claim that shifts perspective.

**Bad core arguments** (topics, not arguments):
- "Expectations matter"
- "The human side of technology"
- "Change management for nonprofits"

**Good core arguments** (specific, reframing, provoke disagreement):
- "The nonprofit tech industry is over-investing in experience and ignoring the lever that actually determines outcomes: expectations"
- "Three Disneyland cast members making $450/day outperformed a $994M ride — because they managed expectations, not experiences"
- "Your CRM implementation failed not because the technology was wrong, but because nobody managed what people expected it to feel like"

```
CORE ARGUMENT
─────────────
DRAFT 1: [First attempt]
DRAFT 2: [Sharper version]
DRAFT 3: [Sharpest — usually the one]

THE ARGUMENT IN ONE SENTENCE:
[This is the sentence Tim should be able to say from memory.
 If he can't say it in one breath, it's not sharp enough.]

REFRAME TEST:
- What does the audience believe BEFORE the talk? [Current frame]
- What should they believe AFTER? [New frame]
- What's the moment of surprise between the two? [The shift]
```

## Step 3: Select the Story Architecture

A keynote needs ONE anchor story that carries the entire argument. Supporting stories are optional and brief.

```
STORY ARCHITECTURE
──────────────────
ANCHOR STORY: [The one story that IS the talk]
- Setup:     [What's the situation? Who are the characters?]
- Tension:   [What goes wrong or what's at stake?]
- Turn:      [The moment that changes everything]
- Landing:   [What it means — but DON'T over-explain]

CONNECTION TO ARGUMENT: [How does this story PROVE the core argument
                         without Tim having to say "and the lesson is..."]

SUPPORTING STORIES (0-2 max):
- [Brief story 1 — what it reinforces]
- [Brief story 2 — what it reinforces]

DATA POINT(S) (0-2 max):
- [Surprising stat that creates an "oh" moment]
```

**Story selection criteria:**
1. Does it carry the argument without explanation?
2. Does it create an emotional moment (surprise, recognition, tension)?
3. Is it Tim's own story or observation? (First-person > third-person > research)
4. Can Tim tell it from memory without notes?
5. Does it have a "turn" — a moment where the meaning shifts?

## Design Rules

1. **Write the close first.** Everything else serves the close. Tim's pattern is amazing open, weak close. Reverse it.
2. **One argument, not three.** Tim's Quickstart 9 generates 5 arguments per minute. Pick the strongest. Kill the rest.
3. **Story carries the argument.** If Tim has to SAY "and the point is..." the story didn't work. Redesign it.
4. **No framework slides.** If a 2x2 matrix or a step-by-step process appears, it's a training. Convert to story or cut.
5. **The one line must be repeatable.** Test: would an audience member text this to a colleague? If not, sharpen it.
6. **Peak in the final third.** Front-loaded talks die at the end. Build toward the peak.
7. **End with the mic drop, not the to-do list.** "5 things to do" is a training close. A keynote close is a single haunting challenge.
8. **Silence is a tool.** After the turn, after the peak, after the close — script the pauses. Tim fills silence with explanation. Don't.

## Tim's Keynote Strengths to Leverage

- **Disneyland story**: Keynote-grade. The niece, the stormtroopers, the rebel spy, the $450 vs. $994M stat. This is a complete talk in one story.
- **The reframe**: "Your tech is fine. Your humans don't know how to human." This is a core argument.
- **Warmth + conviction**: Tim's High I creates genuine connection. Don't bury it under slides.
- **Data surprises**: Tim finds stats that make people lean forward. Use them at the peak, not the preamble.
