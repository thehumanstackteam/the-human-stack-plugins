# Tim Lockie: Instructional Design Profile

## Natural Advantages

**Verbal Reasoning & Communication**
- Articulates complex concepts with clarity and accessibility
- Exceptional big-picture thinking — sees connections others miss
- Story-driven delivery — uses metaphors and narratives to anchor concepts
- High D/High I DISC — direct, enthusiastic, naturally engages audiences

**Operational Style**
- Quick Start 9 (Kolbe) — creates urgency, experiments brilliantly, improvises within structure
- Commands presence and attention, generates momentum
- Works best with permission to move, adapt, and iterate

## Design Challenges (Compensate For These)

**Cognitive Load**
- Low working memory — leans heavily on slides to narrate story
- Risk: becomes slide-focused, loses room energy and audience connection
- Mitigation: design sessions with speaker notes, not slide-dependent structure

**Detail & Logistics**
- Skips critical timing, logistical details, step-by-step instructions
- Activity instructions are conceptual direction, not foolproof clarity
- Rarely creates participant handouts despite brilliant content
- Mitigation: build checklist templates into design; template all materials

**Sequential Thinking**
- Jumps from A to M; assumes B-L are obvious
- References inside context without explanation
- Risk: loses audiences unfamiliar with his framework
- Mitigation: enforce logical step-by-step flows in designs

**Time Estimation**
- Consistently underestimates duration, especially for interaction
- Quick Start 9 generates content faster than reality allows
- Add 30-50% to all his time estimates
- Mitigation: build timing buffers into templates; flag overloaded decks

**Engagement & Facilitation**
- Self-described as "really bad at engagement questions"
- Needs exact scripted questions with timers, not facilitation guidelines
- Mitigation: provide word-for-word prompts with time allocations

**Story & Structure**
- Doesn't naturally create story launchers for each section
- Gets framework-focused, skips the narrative that makes people care
- Amazing openings but weak closes (trails off)
- Mitigation: write close first, design story moments into sections

## Speaking Sweet Spot

Tim thrives when sessions: start with high-energy hook, build from stories/metaphors to framework, include genuine audience interaction, create urgency for change, allow improvisation within structure, avoid reading slides verbatim.

## Common Patterns to Watch

1. **Content Fire Hose** — too many slides/ideas for timeframe
2. **Assume-They-Know** — jargon, skipped steps, inside references
3. **Amazing Opening, Weak Close** — killer hook but trails off
4. **Instruction Ambiguity** — "break into groups and discuss" with no detail
5. **Timing Fantasy** — estimates don't match reality; add buffer
6. **Missing Logistics** — brilliant ideas, missing handouts and materials
7. **Slide Narrator Mode** — over-reliant on slides, loses audience energy
8. **Missing Story Moments** — jumps to framework without narrative hook

## Session Type Guidelines

**Keynotes (30–60 min):** 3–5 big ideas, strong hook and close, one clear CTA. Risk: runs long, too much content.

**Workshops (90 min – 3 hr):** Teach-Practice-Debrief-Transfer cycle with explicit timing. Risk: vague instructions, poor time management, skips debrief.

**Learning Labs (60–90 min):** Frame-Explore-Apply-Synthesize-Plan with clear outcomes. Risk: unclear outcomes, weak structure, overloaded content.

**Webinars/Online (5–10 min modules):** Mix of video, interactive elements, downloadable support. Risk: too much content per module, missing support materials.
